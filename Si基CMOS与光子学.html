    Ge reacts to compressive strain like Si. For tensile strain, the conduction band goes down, and the $\Gamma$ point falles faster than $L$.

  3. Define carrier mobility and explain the importance of channel mobility in CMOS?
    $$
    \mu=\frac{q<\tau>}{m^*}
    $$
    $I_D=W\mu_nQ_I\varepsilon_y$, if you want higher $I_D$, you need higher mobility.

  4. Which methods are used to increase carrier mobility?

    less $m^*$ and higher $<\tau>$

    The second term is governed by less scattering which means less defect density in the material.

    The $m^*$ is engineered by changing the curvature of band, since $m^*=\frac{\hbar^2}{\text d^2 E/dk^2}$

  5. Which strain designs are used in MOSFETs and which design is the best?

    Uniaxial strain is batter than biaxial strain because of the alloy scattering.

    The mobility decreases with increasing of dopant concentration due to carrier-carrier scattering.

    By increasing the dopant concentration the ionized scattering increases as well.

  8. What are pizzo electric and deformation scattering?

    Deformation scattering: Due to vibration of atoms the edge of VB and CB is not well-defined.

    Pizzo electric scattering appears main in conpomd semiconductors e.g. (III-V GaAs, II-VI) where the electron distribution is not uniform. As result the atom experiences a charge oscillation.

    if the atoms in the crystal can be ionized by acoustic phonons.

  9. What are contact resistances?

    The contact resistance appears due to imperfection/ impurities/ defect/ oxide at the contact surface.

  10. What are the criteria to choose materials for silicide formation?

    An appropriate siliside is desired to be formed at low temperature range and consume low amount of Si. The first generation of silicide layers was formed by using Ti, Co, and Pt metals.

    In 65 nm technology node and beyond, Ni was chosen as an outstanding metal for silicide for CMOS structure. NiSi has several advantages compared to other silicides, e.g., TiSi$_2$ and CoSi$_2$ including low sheet resistance on narrow lines as well as low-temperature process with low silicon consumption.

  11. How is silicide formation process for MOSFETs?

    Basic MOSFET structure fabricated -> Metal deposition -> RTP in N$_2$ at 450-700$^\circ C$ (formation of NiSi$_{1-x}$Ge$_x$) -> H$_2$SO$_4$ +H$_2$O$_2$ (wet etch) to remove unreacted metal

  12. When the first nano transistors were fabricated? Explain the architecture.

    In 2003 the first chip from 90 nm node technology was introduced by Intel.

    The architecture consisted of uniaxially compressive strained Si channel when SiGe layers were synthesized in S/D regions.

  13. What are the requirements to integrate new high K materials? Which high k is mostly used nowadays?

    High resistivity, thermally stable, stable interference with silicon, suitable materials for memory devices but not field effect transistors.

    HfO, HfO$_2$, ZrO$_2$.

  14. Explain three methods for inducing strain in nano transistors.

    The methods are categorized into three groups: epitaxy (compressie SiGe at D/S), Nitride stressors, gate material.

  15. What is dopant segregation and explain it from thermodynamic point of view.

    The dopant incorporation is dependent on two fundamental thermo dynamic points: Strain and chemical bonding.

    The first term relates to the difference between the atomic sizes (e.g. Si with Sb or Si with In).

    On the other hand, the second term is quantified by heat sublimation (e.g. Si(40) compared to P(31)) where the heat sublimation should be higher than Si. Then the dopant would incorporate.

  16. What is selective epitaxy and none selective epitaxy? How are they established?

    The selective epitaxy requires Cl-based molecules e.g. SiH$_2$Cl$_2$ and HCl, meanwhile for nonselective mode H-based molecules are demanded e.g. SiH$_4$.

  17. Which type of modes are involved in CVD?

    Two modes: kinetic and mass transport limited modes.

    In kinetic modes: the growth rate is temperature dependent, meanwhile in mass transport mode the growth is controlled by the number (amount) of the molecules during the deposition.

  18. How indirect to direct transition occur in Ge materials?

    GeSn alloy or tense strain + highly N-type doping.

  19. What is type I and type II detectors?

    The light is absorbed and takes an electron from VB to CB, the detector can be composed of direct bandgap material such as Ge/S GeSn.

    The type II design demands a multi layer structure with alternative TS/CS layers, such as TS Ge/ CS GeSn.

  20. What is interband and inter subband lasers?

    The interband laser functions through transition from CB to VB where the layers are direct band matrerial.

    Meanwhile, the intersubband laser operates through the transitions inside the VB (lh & hh bands) or CB ($\Delta 2$ & $\Delta 4$ bands).

    For such laser indirect band gap materials can be used. In this case, the laser should be cooled (70K) to freeze the phonons.

    Interband lasers can operate at room temperature.

  21. How does the first Ge laser operate?

    By inducing tensile strain (1.25 strain) and highly N-Type doping of 7.6$\times 10^{19}\text{cm}^{-3}$ in Ge, An indirect-to-direct transition was achieved.

    This structure (PIN) was used for laser in 2010 by MIT.

  22. What is benefit of SWIR compared to MidIR and LWIR?

    SWIR refers to 1.5-3$\mu$m range which is providing reflective imaging compared to MIDIR and LWIR which is thermal imaging with longer wavelength.
